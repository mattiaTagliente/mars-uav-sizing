SECTION 1: STEADY LEVEL FLIGHT FUNDAMENTALS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SOURCE: Anderson "Introduction to Flight" or Raymer "Aircraft Design"
Check Zotero for these sources.

1.1 FORCE EQUILIBRIUM

In steady level flight, the forces balance:
- Lift = Weight: L = W
- Thrust = Drag: T = D

Present these fundamental equations:
$$L = W$$ {#eq:lift-weight}
$$T = D$$ {#eq:thrust-drag}

CITE: Any introductory flight mechanics textbook

1.2 LIFT EQUATION

The lift equation:
$$L = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 S C_L$$ {#eq:lift}

Where:
- L = lift force (N)
- ρ = air density (kg/m³)
- V = airspeed (m/s)
- S = wing reference area (m²)
- C_L = lift coefficient (dimensionless)

At cruise (L = W):
$$W = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 S C_L$$

Solving for required C_L:
$$C_L = \frac{2W}{\rho V^2 S} = \frac{2(W/S)}{\rho V^2}$$ {#eq:cl-required}

CITE: Anderson or equivalent

1.3 DRAG EQUATION

Total drag:
$$D = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 S C_D$$ {#eq:drag}

Using the parabolic drag polar (from @sec:aerodynamic-analysis):
$$C_D = C_{D0} + \frac{C_L^2}{\pi \cdot AR \cdot e}$$ {#eq:drag-polar}

Where:
- C_D0 = zero-lift drag coefficient
- AR = aspect ratio
- e = Oswald efficiency factor

VALUES (from prompt 02D):
- C_D0 = 0.030
- e = 0.80
- AR = 12 (nominal)

CITE: Raymer or Anderson for drag polar formulation

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: LIFT-TO-DRAG RATIO
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

2.1 L/D FROM DRAG POLAR

The lift-to-drag ratio is:
$$\frac{L}{D} = \frac{C_L}{C_D} = \frac{C_L}{C_{D0} + C_L^2/(\pi \cdot AR \cdot e)}$$ {#eq:ld-ratio}

2.2 MAXIMUM L/D

To find maximum L/D, differentiate and set to zero:
$$\frac{d(L/D)}{dC_L} = 0$$

Result:
$$C_L^* = \sqrt{\pi \cdot AR \cdot e \cdot C_{D0}}$$ {#eq:cl-star}

At this C_L, the induced drag equals the parasite drag.

The maximum L/D is:
$$\left(\frac{L}{D}\right)_{max} = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi \cdot AR \cdot e}{C_{D0}}}$$ {#eq:ld-max}

EXAMPLE CALCULATION:
With AR = 12, e = 0.80, C_D0 = 0.030:
(L/D)_max = 0.5 × sqrt(π × 12 × 0.80 / 0.030)
(L/D)_max = 0.5 × sqrt(1005)
(L/D)_max ≈ 15.9

CITE: Anderson "Fundamentals of Aerodynamics" or Raymer

2.3 SPEED FOR MAXIMUM L/D

The speed at which (L/D)_max occurs:
$$V_{(L/D)max} = \sqrt{\frac{2W}{\rho S C_L^*}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(W/S)}{\rho \sqrt{\pi \cdot AR \cdot e \cdot C_{D0}}}}$$ {#eq:v-ld-max}

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: CRUISE POWER
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3.1 POWER REQUIRED FOR LEVEL FLIGHT

Power required to overcome drag:
$$P_{required} = D \times V = \frac{D \times V}{\eta_{prop}}$$ (shaft power)

For propulsive power:
$$P_{cruise} = \frac{T \times V}{\eta_{prop}} = \frac{D \times V}{\eta_{prop}}$$ {#eq:cruise-power-basic}

Since T = D = W/(L/D):
$$P_{cruise} = \frac{W \times V}{(L/D) \times \eta_{prop}}$$ {#eq:cruise-power}

CITE: Raymer, Anderson, or similar

3.2 ELECTRICAL POWER

Including motor and ESC efficiencies:
$$P_{electric} = \frac{P_{cruise}}{\eta_{motor} \times \eta_{ESC}}$$ {#eq:cruise-electric}

$$P_{electric} = \frac{W \times V}{(L/D) \times \eta_{prop} \times \eta_{motor} \times \eta_{ESC}}$$ {#eq:cruise-electric-full}

Combined propulsive efficiency:
$$\eta_{total} = \eta_{prop} \times \eta_{motor} \times \eta_{ESC}$$

VALUES (from prompt 02C):
- η_prop = 0.55 (Mars low-Re)
- η_motor = 0.85
- η_ESC = 0.95
- η_total = 0.55 × 0.85 × 0.95 = 0.44

3.3 POWER LOADING FORMULATION

Express as power loading (P/W):
$$\frac{P}{W} = \frac{V}{(L/D) \times \eta_{total}}$$ {#eq:power-loading-cruise}

This is the CRUISE CONSTRAINT on the matching chart.

EXAMPLE:
At V = 40 m/s, (L/D) = 15, η_total = 0.44:
P/W = 40 / (15 × 0.44) = 6.06 W/N

For Mars (g = 3.711 m/s²):
P/W = 6.06 × 3.711 = 22.5 W/kg

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: STALL CONSTRAINT
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 STALL SPEED

The minimum flight speed (stall) occurs at maximum C_L:
$$V_{stall} = \sqrt{\frac{2W}{\rho S C_{L,max}}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(W/S)}{\rho C_{L,max}}}$$ {#eq:stall-speed}

CITE: Any aerodynamics textbook

4.2 WING LOADING CONSTRAINT

Rearranging for wing loading:
$$\frac{W}{S} = \frac{1}{2} \rho V_{stall}^2 C_{L,max}$$ {#eq:wing-loading-stall}

For a minimum operating speed V_min with margin:
$$\frac{W}{S} \leq \frac{1}{2} \rho V_{min}^2 C_{L,max}$$ {#eq:wing-loading-constraint}

This is a VERTICAL LINE on the matching chart (W/S upper bound).

VALUES (from prompts 02D, 02I):
- C_L,max = 1.0 (at Re = 60,000)
- ρ = 0.020 kg/m³
- V_min = 1.2 × V_stall (assume V_stall ≈ 25 m/s for estimate)

EXAMPLE:
W/S ≤ 0.5 × 0.020 × 25² × 1.0 = 6.25 N/m² = 6.25 Pa

This is VERY LOW compared to Earth aircraft (typical: 1500-5000 Pa)

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 5: ENDURANCE ANALYSIS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5.1 AVAILABLE ENERGY

From battery (same as rotorcraft):
$$E_{available} = f_{batt} \times MTOW \times e_{spec} \times DoD \times \eta_{batt}$$ {#eq:energy-available}

5.2 ENDURANCE EQUATION

Endurance for electric aircraft at constant speed:
$$t = \frac{E_{available}}{P_{electric}}$$ {#eq:endurance-basic}

Substituting:
$$t = \frac{f_{batt} \times MTOW \times e_{spec} \times DoD \times \eta_{batt}}{W \times V / ((L/D) \times \eta_{total})}$$

Since W = MTOW × g:
$$t = \frac{f_{batt} \times e_{spec} \times DoD \times \eta_{batt} \times (L/D) \times \eta_{total}}{g \times V}$$ {#eq:endurance-fixedwing}

This is the KEY ENDURANCE EQUATION for fixed-wing.

Note: Endurance is INDEPENDENT of MTOW (for fixed fractions).

SECTION 6: THE TAKEOFF PROBLEM
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

THIS IS WHY FIXED-WING FAILS FOR MARS MISSION.

6.1 TAKEOFF GROUND ROLL

The takeoff ground roll distance:
$$S_{TO} = \frac{V_{TO}^2}{2 \bar{a}}$$ {#eq:takeoff-roll}

Where:
- V_TO = takeoff speed ≈ 1.1 × V_stall
- ā = average acceleration during ground roll

Average acceleration:
$$\bar{a} = \frac{g}{W} \left[ T - D - \mu_r (W - L) \right]_{avg}$$ {#eq:takeoff-accel}

CITE: Raymer Chapter 17 "Takeoff and Landing"

6.2 MARS-SPECIFIC ISSUES

On Mars, the takeoff roll is extremely long because:

a) LOW DENSITY:
   - V_stall is higher (∝ 1/√ρ)
   - At ρ = 0.020 kg/m³ vs Earth 1.225 kg/m³
   - V_stall increases by √(1.225/0.020) = √61 ≈ 7.8×
   - If Earth V_stall = 25 m/s → Mars V_stall ≈ 200 m/s (impossible!)

   Note: This calculation assumes same W/S. Mars UAV must have MUCH lower W/S.

b) RECALCULATION WITH MARS-APPROPRIATE W/S:
   For W/S = 6.25 Pa (from stall constraint), V_stall ≈ 25 m/s
   V_TO ≈ 1.1 × 25 = 27.5 m/s
   
c) ACCELERATION IS LOW:
   - Lower density → lower thrust (propeller)
   - Lower friction coefficient (μ_r ≈ 0.02-0.05 on regolith)
   - Net: acceleration is low

d) ESTIMATED GROUND ROLL:
   With T/W = 0.3, rough estimate:
   ā ≈ 0.2 × g_Mars = 0.74 m/s²
   S_TO = (27.5)² / (2 × 0.74) = 756 / 1.48 ≈ 511 m
   
   This is HUNDREDS OF METERS with no runway infrastructure.

6.3 ALTERNATIVE LAUNCH METHODS

CATAPULT/RAIL LAUNCH:
- Requires ground infrastructure
- Not available from habitat
- Adds mission complexity

ROCKET-ASSISTED TAKEOFF (RATO):
- Solid rocket boosters add mass
- One-time use → multiple flights impractical
- Safety hazard near habitat

BALLOON-DROP LAUNCH:
- Deploy from high-altitude balloon
- Extremely complex for Mars
- No balloon infrastructure exists

AIR-LAUNCH FROM LARGER VEHICLE:
- Not applicable (no carrier aircraft on Mars)

CITE: Any sources discussing alternative launch methods if available.

6.4 LANDING PROBLEM

Landing also requires:
- Long rollout distance after touchdown
- Prepared surface to avoid obstacles
- Same issues as takeoff

7.2 CONCLUSION

The pure fixed-wing configuration CAN meet the endurance and range
requirements due to its high aerodynamic efficiency ((L/D) ≈ 15).

However, it CANNOT satisfy the VTOL requirement. Conventional takeoff
and landing requires a prepared runway of hundreds of meters, which does
not exist on Mars.

The fixed-wing configuration is **NOT FEASIBLE** for the Mars UAV mission
due to infrastructure constraints, not aerodynamic limitations.
